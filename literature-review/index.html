Sources are grouped by the role they play in the argument. Each entry carries a one-line note on how the study uses it. Links were resolving as of the updated date above.

Foundational theory

Charland, Maurice. “Constitutive Rhetoric: The Case of the Peuple Québécois.” Quarterly Journal of Speech 73, no. 2 (1987): 133–150. doi.org/10.1080/00335638709383799 The master apparatus for the whole project: rhetoric calls a “people” into being and embeds a telos in the act of constituting it.
Burke, Kenneth. A Rhetoric of Motives. Berkeley: University of California Press, 1950. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Rhetoric_of_Motives Supplies identification (consubstantiality), the first of the two engines Charland fuses into constitutive rhetoric.
Althusser, Louis. “Ideology and Ideological State Apparatuses.” In Lenin and Philosophy and Other Essays, 1970. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideology_and_Ideological_State_Apparatuses Supplies interpellation, the “hailing” by which a discourse seats a subject in a position before that subject can refuse it.
McGee, Michael Calvin. “In Search of ‘the People’: A Rhetorical Alternative.” Quarterly Journal of Speech 61, no. 3 (1975): 235–249. doi.org/10.1080/00335637509383289 Establishes “the people” as a rhetorical achievement rather than a pre-existing fact, the premise the three cases stress-test.

The Québec case

“1980 Quebec referendum.” Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1980_Quebec_referendum The first sovereignty-association vote and the immediate context for Charland’s 1987 analysis; defeated 59.6% to 40.4%.
“1995 Referendum on Québec’s Accession to Sovereignty.” Élections Québec. electionsquebec.qc.ca Official results for the near-miss of October 30, 1995 (No 50.58%, Yes 49.42%): the case where a thick ethnos came one point from a forward telos.
Grand Council of the Crees. Sovereign Injustice (1995); see also “Grand Council of the Crees.” Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Council_of_the_Crees Records the Cree counter-referendum of October 24, 1995 (96.3% to remain in Canada): a prior people refusing constitution into someone else’s “people.”
Supreme Court of Canada. Reference re Secession of Quebec, [1998] 2 SCR 217. decisions.scc-csc.ca/.../item/1643 Holds that unilateral secession is unconstitutional but that a clear majority on a clear question obliges negotiation: the juridical frame both later cases run into.

The Alberta 2026 case

“2026 Alberta referendum.” Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Alberta_referendum Overview of the October 19, 2026 multi-question referendum and the separation question’s path onto the ballot.
Trynacity, Kim. “Alberta premier, cabinet formalize wording of Oct. 19 separation question.” CBC News, 2026. cbc.ca/news/canada/edmonton/...9.7215485 Primary source for the exact ballot wording: a two-option, non-binding question about commencing a legal process, not a direct vote to separate.
Smith, Danielle. “Premier’s Address to the Province.” Alberta.ca, 2025. alberta.ca/premiers-address-to-the-province The government’s own framing of grievance and identity, read as the constitutive appeal under analysis.
“Alberta Sovereignty within a United Canada Act,” SA 2022. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alberta_Sovereignty_within_a_United_Canada_Act The title itself is the evidence for the detachable telos: a sovereignty claim held expressly “within a united Canada.”
Wakefield, Jonny. “Judge overturns Elections Alberta’s approval of separation referendum petition.” CBC News, May 13, 2026. cbc.ca/news/canada/edmonton/...9.7198553 Reports the King’s Bench ruling (Justice Shaina Leonard) quashing the petition for failure of the Crown’s duty to consult.
JFK Law. “Treaty Rights and Alberta Separatism: The Crown’s Duty to Consult in Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation v Alberta (Chief Electoral Officer).” 2026. jfklaw.ca/treaty-rights-and-alberta-separatism… Legal analysis of the ruling: separation would adversely affect Treaty 7 and 8 rights, so the electoral step itself triggered the duty to consult.
“Numbered Treaties.” Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numbered_Treaties Establishes that Treaties 6, 7, and 8 (1876–1899) predate Alberta’s creation in 1905: a prior peoplehood with a standing juridical claim.
Angus Reid Institute. “Alberta Separation: Three-in-five say they’d vote in October to stay, but half say the question is ‘confusing.’” 2026. angusreid.org/alberta-referendum-question Polling showing the identity and the secessionist conclusion come apart: roughly 60% would vote No on the official question (about 35% Yes), and 67% choose to stay on a plain leave/stay framing.

The US “true America” framing

“No Kings protests.” Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_Kings_protests Documents the 2025–2026 mobilizations whose “restore the real America” register supplies the restorationist (backward-vector) case.
“3.5% rule.” Wikipedia. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3.5%_rule The threshold claim the movement invokes about itself; used here as a self-constituting number, a “people” asserting its own scale.
Chenoweth, Erica. “The 3.5% Rule: Understanding What Makes Protest Powerful.” Harvard Kennedy School. hks.harvard.edu/.../35-rule The scholarship behind the figure (Chenoweth and Stephan), distinguishing the empirical finding from its rhetorical redeployment.
